Quadratic Formula

Where:

  • x = the variable representing the solutions
  • a = the coefficient of x²
  • b = the coefficient of x
  • c = the constant term
  • ± indicates that there are generally two solutions: one for addition and one for subtraction
This formula is used to solve quadratic equations, which are equations whose highest power is 2.
They are in the form: ax² + bx + c = 0, where a ≠ 0.
Example:
Find the values of x for which 2x² = 14x + 36
Solution:
This must be re-arranged to give 2x² - 14x - 36 = 0
Here, a = 2; b = -14; c = -36. Take note that the signs (positive or negative) matter.
Using the formula, we'd have this.
Solving this, we'd get x = 3.5 ± 5.5
That gives x = 9 or x = -2

An easier method might be breaking down the values.
In the equation above, you will notice that a, b, c, 0 are multiples of 2, so we can factor 2 out to get x² - 7x - 18 = 0.
This way, we get to work with smaller values, which can make your work seem easier. Either way, you'll get the same answer.
To check your answers, you can substitute these values for x in the equation. If the equation aligns, the values are correct; if it doesn't, there's something wrong with your work.

Difference of Squares

a² - b² = (a + b)(a - b)
Where:
a and b are just algebraic expressions.
This is majorly used in algebra problems. It can really make problems simpler.
Example:
Solve: 38² - 32²
Solution:
This can be changed to: (38 - 32)(38 + 32)
That way, you get: (6)(70) = 420
Isn't that much easier than finding the individual squares and subtracting?

Factored Form of a Quadratic

y = a(x - p)(x - q)
Where:

  • y is the dependent variable.
  • a is a coefficient that affects the width and direction of the parabola.
  • p and q are the roots (or zeros) of the quadratic equation.
Example:
Find the factored form of this quadratic equation: f(x) = x² - 5x + 6
Solution:
To find the roots, you can set the quadratic equation to zero:
x² - 5x + 6 = 0
Using the quadratic formula, you can determine that the roots are: x = 2 and x = 3.
Therefore, the factored form of the quadratic equation is:
f(x) = a(x - 2)(x - 3)
That means: x² - 5x + 6 = a(x - 2)(x - 3)
Hence x² - 5x + 6 = a(x² - 5x + 6)
Thus a = 1, so the final factored form is:
f(x) = (x - 2)(x - 3)
You can always expand this form to check if it matches the original quadratic equation.

Compound Interest Formula

Where:

  • A = the total amount of money accumulated after n years, including interest.
  • P = the principal amount (the initial amount of money).
  • r = the annual interest rate (decimal).
  • n = the number of times that interest is compounded per year.
  • t = the number of years the money is invested or borrowed for.
This formula is used to calculate the total amount of money accumulated after a certain period, considering compound interest.
Example:
If you invest $1000 at an annual interest rate of 5% compounded quarterly for 3 years, what will be the total amount?
Solution:
Here, P = 1000, r = 0.05, n = 4, and t = 3.
Plugging these values into the formula:
A = 1000(1 + 0.05/4)^(4*3)
A = 1000(1 + 0.0125)^(12)
A ≈ 1000(1.1616) ≈ $1161.62

So, after 3 years, you would have approximately $1161.62.

Slope-Intercept Formula

The slope-intercept form of a linear equation is expressed as:
y = mx + b
Where:

  • y = the dependent variable (output).
  • m = the slope of the line (rise over run).
  • x = the independent variable (input).
  • b = the y-intercept (the value of y when x = 0).
This formula is used to express a linear equation in a way that makes it easy to identify the slope and y-intercept of the line.
You should note that coordinates are written as: (x, y)
Note: The y-intercept occurs when the value of the x-coordinate is 0; the x-intercept occurs when the value of the y-coordinate is 0.
Example:
If a line has a slope of 2 and a y-intercept of -3, what is the equation of the line?
Solution:
Using the slope-intercept form:
y = 2x - 3
This means that for every unit increase in x, y increases by 2, and when x = 0, y = -3.

Standard Form of a Circle Equation

The standard form of a circle's equation is expressed as:
(x - h)² + (y - k)² = r²
Where:

  • h = the x-coordinate of the center of the circle.
  • k = the y-coordinate of the center of the circle.
  • r = the radius of the circle.
This formula is used to represent a circle in a way that makes it easy to identify its center and radius. If you have a different form of the circle's equation, it can often be converted to this standard form by completing the square.
Example:
If a circle has a center at (3, 4) and a radius of 5, what is the equation of the circle?
Solution:
Using the standard form:
(x - 3)² + (y - 4)² = 5²
Simplifying this gives:
(x - 3)² + (y - 4)² = 25
This means that any point (x, y) that satisfies this equation lies on the circumference of the circle.

Average Formula

Where:

  • The sum of all values is the total obtained by adding all the numbers in the set.
  • The number of values is the count of how many numbers are in the set.
This formula is used to find the central tendency of a set of numbers.
Example:
Find the average of the numbers 4, 8, 12, and 16.
Solution:
First, calculate the sum: 4 + 8 + 12 + 16 = 40
Then, count the number of values: There are 4 numbers.
Now, apply the formula:
Average = 40 / 4 = 10
So, the average of these numbers is 10.
It's that simple!

Distance Formula

Where:

  • d = the distance between two points.
  • (x₁, y₁) = the coordinates of the first point.
  • (x₂, y₂) = the coordinates of the second point.
This formula is used to calculate the straight-line distance between two points in a Cartesian coordinate system.
Example:
Find the distance between the points (3, 4) and (7, 1).
Solution:
Using the formula:
d = √((7 - 3)² + (1 - 4)²)
d = √(4² + (-3)²)
d = √(16 + 9)
d = √25
d = 5
So, the distance between these two points is 5 units.

Exponential Growth or Decay

Where:

  • A = the quantity at time t.
  • A₀ = the initial quantity (at time t = 0).
  • r = the growth (or decay) rate (as a decimal).
  • n = time(t)/interval
This formula is used to model situations where a quantity grows or decays exponentially over time.
Example:
If a population of bacteria doubles every 3 hours, and you start with 100 bacteria, how many will there be after 9 hours?
Solution:
Here, A₀ = 100, r = 1 (since it doubles), interval = 3, and t = 9.
The number of growth periods (n) in 9 hours is 9 / 3 = 3.
Using the formula:
A = 100 * (2)^(9/3)
A = 100 * (2)^3
A = 100 * 8
A = 800

So, after 9 hours, there will be 800 bacteria.
This formula can also be used for decay by using a decay rate instead of a growth rate.

Simple Probability Formula

Where:

  • P(E) = the probability of an event E occurring.
This formula is used to calculate the likelihood of a specific event occurring.
Example:
If you roll a fair six-sided die, what is the probability of rolling a 4?
Solution:
Here, there is 1 favorable outcome (rolling a 4) and 6 possible outcomes (1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6).
Using the formula:
P(E) = n(E) / n(S) = 1 / 6
So, the probability of rolling a 4 is approximately 0.1667 or 16.67%.
